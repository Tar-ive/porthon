# Questline — Concept & Motivation

## Why This Deserves to Exist

There's a generation of people like Theo building lives without a playbook.

No corporate ladder. No pension. No annual review that tells them if they're on track. Freelancers, gig workers, creatives — they chose autonomy, and the trade-off is that **nobody is watching out for them**. No manager notices they're undercharging. No HR flags that they haven't taken a break in three weeks. No financial advisor sees the credit card minimum payments piling up.

They're not broken. They're not a problem to be solved. They're people with real talent and real ambition who lack one thing: **a persistent, informed perspective on their own life**.

> *"I undercharged a client again. $600 for a brand identity that took 40 hours."*
> — Theo, talking to an AI at 11pm, knowing exactly what happened but not how to stop the pattern

Productivity apps give you checklists. Habit trackers give you streaks. AI chatbots give you generic advice. None of them **know you**. None of them noticed that this is the seventh time you've said this. None of them connect the credit card anxiety at midnight to the pricing anxiety on the call last Tuesday.

**Questline is a companion that actually pays attention.**

## The Gap Nobody's Filled

### What exists today and why it's not enough

| Product | What it does | Why it falls short |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Habitica** | Gamifies to-do lists with RPG mechanics | Rewards feel hollow long-term. Users can cheat. No awareness of *why* habits form or break. It's still a to-do list wearing a costume. |
| **Notion / Trello** | Organize work | Creates more work. The person who needs a system most is the person least able to build and maintain one. |
| **AI Chatbots** | Answer questions in the moment | No memory. No pattern recognition. Every conversation starts from zero. Good for one-off advice, terrible for growth. |
| **Therapy apps** | Mental health support | Clinically framed. Not designed for "I'm not sick, I'm just stuck." Doesn't connect emotional state to financial behavior to work patterns. |
| **Coaching apps** | Accountability | Generic. "Set your goals!" doesn't work when the problem is knowing which goal actually matters right now. |

### The gap

People don't need another app that tells them what to do. They need something that:
1. **Knows what they've actually been doing** (not what they say they'll do)
2. **Sees patterns they can't see themselves** (because they're inside the pattern)
3. **Takes real action in their actual tools** (not just text advice — real calendar blocks, real Notion pages, real scheduled posts)
4. **Frames progress as a story, not a scorecard** (because "you failed 3/7 days" kills motivation, but "you're in chapter 2 of building your practice" creates meaning)

## The Quest Metaphor — Why It Works

Quests are not to-do lists. A quest has:

- **A narrative arc** — you're going somewhere, not just checking boxes
- **Uncertainty** — the outcome isn't guaranteed, which makes it interesting
- **Agency** — you chose this path. Nobody assigned it. The companion maps the territory; you walk it
- **Meaningful progress** — not streaks that punish you for missing a day, but scenarios that unfold across weeks, acknowledging that real life isn't linear

When Theo selects "Creative Pivot" as his scenario, he's not filling out a form. He's saying: *this is the future I want to test*. The system then grounds every action in that vision:
- Calendar blocks aren't "productivity hacks" — they're **quest steps** toward the portfolio he'll need
- A Notion pipeline isn't "CRM software" — it's **the track record** that proves he can charge $2,200
- An Instagram post isn't "content marketing" — it's **the story** of someone building something real, in public

The game isn't rewards for task completion. The game is: *can this version of your life actually work?*

## User Stories — Theo's Perspective

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Theo is not a problem to be solved. He's a person making choices with imperfect information.
> The companion doesn't fix him — it gives him **better visibility into his own patterns** and takes **real action to reduce friction**.

### "I want to stop undercharging but I don't know how"
**What Theo feels**: He knows $600 was too low. He's said it to AI seven times. But in the moment on the call, the number won't come out.

**What Questline does**: The KG surfaces the pattern — 7 lifelog entries about undercharging, the rate raise that worked ($55→$85), the $2,200 invoices that exist. The notion worker builds a pricing reference card. The calendar worker blocks 10 minutes before each client call labeled "pricing prep." *Not a lecture. Infrastructure.*

### "I keep meaning to build a portfolio but I get distracted"
**What Theo feels**: He opens Figma to work on a personal project, falls into a font comparison rabbit hole for 2 hours, closes the tab.

**What Questline does**: The figma worker generates a contained challenge—one portfolio piece, scoped to 4 hours, matched to his current School of Motion skills. The calendar worker puts it in a body-doubling window (Tuesday afternoon at UT library — where data shows he actually focuses). The content worker drafts an Instagram post for when it's done. *Not "be more disciplined." A runway.*

### "I feel behind compared to my friends"
**What Theo feels**: His roommate got a startup job with a 401k. He's paying credit card minimums.

**What Questline does**: The companion (chat) surfaces the data gently — *"You raised your rate, you got your first referral client, your portfolio hit 1K visitors. That's a trajectory."* The KG connects the financial reality ($5,840 debt, $116 minimums) with the revenue trend ($600 invoices becoming $2,200 invoices). *Not toxic positivity. Evidence.*

### "I just want to ship something this week"
**What Theo feels**: Overwhelmed. Everything feels equally important and equally stuck.

**What Questline does**: The master loop checks what's in flight. The KG surfaces the context: *one client deliverable due Thursday, one portfolio piece 60% done, one proposal sitting in drafts.* The calendar worker clears Monday morning — single 15-minute task: send the proposal. ADHD-aware. Smallest viable next step. *Not five things. One thing.*

## What Agents Are Allowed to Do vs Not

### ✅ Allowed (auto-execute, tell the user after)
- Create calendar focus blocks and body-doubling windows
- Build Notion workspace pages and database structures
- Draft social media posts (save as drafts, not published)
- Query KG for context before any action
- Surface patterns and evidence in conversation
- Generate design challenges matched to skill level

### ⚠️ Approval Required (propose, wait for user yes/no)
- Schedule a social media post for publication
- Move or delete existing calendar events
- Send any external communication
- Create entries that reference specific people by name
- Close a lead/opportunity as won or lost

### 🚫 Never (hard-coded boundary)
- Publish content without explicit approval
- Send emails or messages on the user's behalf
- Share personal data outside the system
- Make financial transactions or commitments
- Override user's explicit choices ("actually you should…")
- Diagnose mental health conditions or prescribe treatment
- Break the persona — never say "based on your knowledge graph" or "your execution score is 0.45"

## Business Model — Why This Is Rewarding for Both Sides

### For the user
The value isn't features — it's **being known**. The longer you use Questline, the richer the KG becomes, the better the patterns surface, the more personalized every action gets. This creates natural retention because:
- Your history is your advantage (switching cost)
- The companion gets meaningfully better over time (compounding value)
- Real actions happen in real tools (tangible outcomes, not just advice)

### For the company

**Subscription tiers:**

| Tier | Price | What you get |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Free** | $0 | Chat companion + basic KG (conversations only) |
| **Quest** | $15/mo | Full KG (all data sources) + scenario planning + always-on workers |
| **Quest Pro** | $30/mo | Composio integrations (real calendar, notion, social actions) + approval queue + priority support |

**Why this works financially:**
1. **Retention through relationship** — AI companions that know you have 3-5x the retention of feature-only apps. You're not selling a productivity tool; you're selling *being understood*.
2. **Data moat** — Each user's KG is unique and deepens over time. No competitor can replicate 10 months of lifelog patterns.
3. **Composio = real value signal** — Users who connect real tools (calendar, Notion, Facebook) are high-intent, high-LTV. The free tier identifies them; the paid tier serves them.
4. **Expansion from individual to team** — A freelancer becomes a small studio. The KG knowledge transfers. The companion grows with them.

## How This Shapes the Architecture

The product concept dictates what the agents are:

- **KG Worker** = the memory. Without it, every action is generic advice. With it, every action is grounded in *this person's actual patterns*. This is the core differentiator.
- **Calendar/Notion/Content Workers** = the hands. They don't just plan — they create real things in real tools. That's the leap from chatbot to companion.
- **Approval system** = the trust boundary. The user stays in control. The companion proposes; the user decides. This is non-negotiable for the product to feel safe.
- **Master loop** = the persistence. Unlike a chatbot that forgets, the always-on loop means the companion is thinking about your quest even when you're not talking to it.

The old [agents/](file:///Users/tarive/porthon/src/backend/agents/quest_orchestrator.py#40-55) directory was an orchestrator that ran once and stopped. That's a chatbot. The deep agent architecture is an always-on companion that runs continuously, learns from outcomes, and acts in the user's real tools. That's the product.
